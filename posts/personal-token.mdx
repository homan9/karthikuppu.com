Many of our greatest ideas were discovered after years, if not decades, of fruitless, messy explorations. Years spent following innate curiosities that led to dead ends. Years honing crafts without results.

But we're terrible at supporting such journeys that come before great ideas.

_After_ you discover[^direction] a great idea, you can start a company for it. By sharing equity in your company you can attract strong collaborators, and by selling equity in your company you can raise venture capital and share your financial risk with others.

[^direction]: Or a reasonably clear direction toward one.

The structure of a company enabled us to work on more difficult and ambitious ideas by aligning incentives. It's no wonder why many of the greatest ideas reach us through companies.

But _before_ you discover an idea, there's no way to tangibly incentivize others to help you on your exploration, on your journey of life itself. As a result, the majority of difficult explorations – the kind that would lead to discovering many profound ideas – never even get started. This hurts us far more than we can imagine.

---

What if we could align incentives for our explorations in the way that we can for building ideas? What if we could share _equity_ in our future success with others, across everything we may ever work on, to incentivize people to help us on our journey itself?

Let's say we have an instrument that turns your success into a form that resembles a company. Like a company, you would have shares. Your shareholders would have a proportional claim on the wealth you earn when you sell your stakes in companies (and similar assets with extreme upside potential).

<CapTable caption="Jane's cap table." />

Shareholding is strictly scoped to extreme upside and is not a claim on your income [^ISA]. When you win, your shareholders win. But if you don't succeed, you wouldn't be in a worse off position (like in debt).

[^ISA]: This is what makes this fundamentally different from ISAs (income share agreements).

This would enable you to share equity in your potential success. By sharing equity, you can attract collaborators and mentors who can help you on your exploration – even _before_ you discover anything. And by selling a stake in your success, you can raise venture capital to finance exploration and experimentation independent of any specific idea or direction.

Even more fundamentally, sharing equity in our success across life enables us to incentivize people to help us succeed in _life_ itself. Our shareholders are those who believe in us beyond any specific idea we may work on.

It takes a village. Each of us required the help and support of many others to enable us to get to where we are today. Family, friends, teachers, mentors, collaborators – it takes a village to raise a person. When we can _tangibly_ share our future success with others, we can incentivize more people, more strongly, to enable us to realize our potential. 

By sharing a stake in our success with the right people, we accelerate and amplify our success in life.

It's not about the money. As any good founder knows, the ability to incentivize great people to work with you is far more important than the venture capital you can raise[^abundance]. The same principle applies to our own cap tables. Sharing equity in our success turns our life into a collaboration. Regardless of how wealthy someone is, as long as they are still curious and want to keep exploring and creating, they would want to curate and grow their village.

[^abundance]: I think this becomes even more true as we move toward greater abundance. As capital becomes more abundant, shareholders will need to bring more to the table than just capital.

Today, only founders can share equity and incentivize support. When each of us can share a stake in our success across everything we may do, those who may not start companies can also incentivize people to help them succeed.

This is because the value of your shares is grounded in your stakes in companies (and similar assets). You can earn such stakes by starting companies, but also by working at them, or even investing in them. Therefore, anyone on the path to creating value through _ownership_ – whether they are founders or engineers or designers or researchers or artists or creators – can incentivize support to help them succeed on their journey. Increasingly, this will be everyone [^everyone].

[^everyone]: From artists and creators to politicians, owning equity is increasingly the path to generate wealth, not income or revenue, etc. As this trend continues, more people would be able to share their future potential success to incentivize others to help them succeed.

---

What exactly is this instrument? Like a company, this would be a legal instrument at its core – an agreement between individuals and their shareholders. Let's call this instrument the "Personal Token" for now.

When you launch your personal token, it would have an initial number of shares. Like companies, you would allocate equity in your personal token in rounds (pre-seed, seed, etc.).

While the personal token resembles a company, it is fundamentally different. It's more personal, more human. And therefore it requires a unique set of features in order to be an instrument that we can actually use.

1. **You aren't owned or controlled by your shareholders**. Your shareholders wouldn't have any influence or control over your life. Shareholding is not ownership. That would be absurd. Your shareholders only have a proportional _economic claim_ to the wealth you will create across your life (from selling stakes in companies and similar assets with extreme upside potential).

2. **You are not harmed in the case of failure**. In the case where you don't go onto create wealth through equity stakes, you wouldn't owe anyone anything. Your income cannot be touched because it is out of scope.

3. **You don't require anyone's permission to share equity in your success**. Shareholding is only a claim on your net profit from selling your stakes in companies (and similar assets), _without exposing the assets themselves_. This means that you don't need anyone's permission to share equity in your personal token.

4. **You control who can hold your shares**. Your cap table is a curated space. Liquidity is permissioned. You can prevent secondary sales to people you don't want to share your success with.

5. **You can buy back your shares**. A misallocated cap table can be detrimental to your success. Since the personal token represents a long time horizon (your life!), it's critical that you have a way to fix and adapt your cap table. For example, if a shareholder becomes toxic or actively harms your success through their association with you, you can buy back your shares at the current market value.

6. **You can carve out a safety net.** Sharing equity in our success can feel daunting if we don't have a safety net. You can define a *floor* – a portion of the wealth you create that is excluded from shareholder claims. For example, with a floor of $30M, the first $30M you make is entirely yours (subject to taxes, etc.), and shareholders' claims only apply on your wealth beyond your floor.

7. **You can reinvest your profits rather than distribute them**. Holding equity in a company is essentially grounded as a claim on the company's profits across its life. Then why do most growing companies not distribute their profits to their shareholders as dividends? This is because reinvesting profits is often better for both the company and its shareholders – who will go onto profit through selling shares at a profit rather than by accumulating dividends. The same principle would apply for people. If your shareholders believe that reinvesting your upside back into yourself to fuel your growth will amplify your success even more, then you should be able to reinvest your wealth and not distribute it. Like with companies, your shareholders would primarily profit by selling shares, not dividends.

---

The personal token puts a lot of power in the hands of the person sharing equity in their success. I think that's a good thing.

**More power to creators is better for both the creators _and_ their shareholders.**

This is a lesson we've learned with companies. A couple of decades ago it was common to see investors polluting companies. They would force their strategies and even force founders out of the companies they started. Over time, founders (and great investors) fought to give founders greater control over their companies. As a result, companies have done much much better, leading to greater returns for everyone involved.

That being said, the personal token must also make sense for shareholders. So that we can incentivize them to help us succeed.

---

How would a personal token shareholder profit?

Let's say you invest in Alice in their seed round as one of their first shareholders. As Alice grows – as she shares her work and ideas, garners a greater following, makes progress, and generally demonstrates greater potential to the world – the world will value her more greatly. In other words, as Alice's _valuation_ increases, you can sell your shares of Alice at a profit.

But this requires you to be able to sell your shares. Companies have an established path toward liquidity. Companies advance through stages of liquidity – pre-seed, seed, A, B, C, etc. – all the way to an IPO. Through these stages, it becomes progressively easier for shareholders to sell their shares in the company.

What is the path to liquidity for a personal token? Why would a person want to enable liquidity for their shares?

To answer these questions, we have to understand the nature of the person who would want to share their success with others. The ideal candidate to launch their personal token is someone who is ambitious, curious, and long-term oriented. They would want to play long-term games with long-term people.

Such people would want to enable their shareholders to win. The more they create success for their shareholders, and the better they treat their shareholders, the more (and better) shareholders they will be able to attract over the course of their life. This same principle applies to startup founders as well. Those who want to build long fruitful careers will care deeply about their reputation.

Enabling liquidity will be an important factor in building a strong reputation. Someone who keeps rejecting secondary sales without good reason, who abuses their power and makes life difficult for their shareholders, will find it difficult to attract support.

I don't expect liquidity for personal tokens to follow the same stages as it does for companies. I can't yet imagine a complete IPO style free trading for personal token shares [^IPO] , but I do expect people to create liquidity pools to enable more seamless trading of their shares over time. By using programmatic rules and AI, people can curated sophisticated liquidity pools that are aligned with who they want to incentivize to help them succeed.

[^IPO]: But who knows! Maybe a certain percentage of outstanding shares may be freely traded like public company shares while another class of shares are more permissioned. I can't imagine full IPO scenario because I, and I expect many others, would want to have some control over who we create wealth for. I would not want people I'm fundamentally misaligned with to ever hold my shares because that would mean that I'm directly creating wealth for them. The more personal nature of sharing our success will lead to different patterns of use.

What about if a person raises money on lies and runs away with it? That would be fraud and shareholders would litigate. This is one of the reasons why the personal token must be a compliant, legally grounded instrument. We need the help of courts to deal with fraud in the way they do for companies.

Outside of outright fraud, the market can handle it. People who operate their personal token in unskillful ways will find that the demand for their shares will fall. They will find it more and more difficult to attract collaborators and build a strong village that will enable them to realize their potential.

Investing in people will come with risk. The power law will exist for people as it does for companies. In many cases, investors will need to write off their investments and move on just as they do with companies.

That being said, it's important to design features that _incentivize_ good behavior. One such feature is royalties. Whenever a shareholder makes a (net) profit selling your shares, you would earn back a small percentage of the shares involved in that transaction. This would incentivize people to enable liquidity and grow. There are many more features like this to explore.

---

How would a person be valued?

Personal tokens, like companies, would have a notion of a _valuation_: the total market capitalization of its shares (i.e, the price per share x number of outstanding shares).

A person's valuation reflects their _potential_ to create value. The greater a person's potential, the greater the gap between their valuation and their current "net worth" (total value of their holdings).

A person's valuation is updated when their shares are transacted. This means that it's possible for valuations to be quite disconnected from reality. The more rare transactions are, the more disconnected a valuation will become from reality. This happens with startups as well. There are many "dead" startups that still haven't shut down and thus carry the valuation from their last fundraising round. But everyone knows that they are basically worthless. Similarly, the best startups are incredibly under-valued because the valuation from their last fundraising round doesn't reflect their recent growth and rise in potential.

What's interesting is that this sort of potential is completely ignored by our markets! A person's net worth does not take into account the future ideas that they will hold equity in (that they themselves may not know!). The personal token would enable a market for a human's _potential_ to create value.

---

There are legitimate concerns. As we see with companies, poisonous shareholders can exert a great deal of pressure on founders to accelerate growth even if that may not be healthy for the founders, or the right thing to do. I've seen situations in which investor pressure has negatively impacted mental health. With personal tokens, such pressure could emerge for life itself, not just a single company. We will need to design the right expectations, features, and culture to ensure that sharing equity in your upside cannot harm you.

If we can address these concerns well, I believe personal tokens will greatly improve our world.

**Personal tokens help democratize opportunity.** Most people with potential don't have access to the support and resources to enable them to realize their potential. When we can invest in people in the way we can invest in companies, we will have a strong incentive to discover, invest and support those with great potential, who are currently overlooked and under-resourced.

**Personal tokens enable a better education system.** Instead of charging money to teach, teachers would _invest_ in students and train them. This aligns incentives toward actually training students in a way that enables them to create value for others in the world – which is what most students want to be able to do! This enables students to share the risk of their education with their teachers rather than be crushed with debt in case their education doesn't lead to outcomes. This incentivizes long-term teaching. As long as a teacher is a shareholder, they are incentivized to provide support, no matter how many years have passed. This enables the best teachers to actually capture the value they help create. In such a world, great teachers will be among the wealthiest in the world – as they should be! This prunes out bad teachers who will go broke because they can't meaningfully impact outcomes. This enables students to have better, more truthful, information about how good a teacher actually is. They can inspect a teacher's track record by inspecting their personal token history in the way they can inspect a company's history through its filings.

**Personal tokens better reflect truth.** There is no such thing as individual success. Great companies are often quite diluted reflecting the truth that it takes a lot of collaboration to build a great company. Similarly, great people will be quite diluted, reflecting the truth that it takes a village to enable an individual to succeed.

**Personal tokens encourage a positive-sum culture.** When we hold equity in others' success, we are incentivized to be more helpful and supportive because the financial incentives finally reflect the truth that we are in this game of life _with_ each other, not against. Imagine the sort of culture that would emerge within a group of people in which each person has a stake in everyone else's success.

**Personal tokens empower younger people**. If everyone had a personal token, then many young people today would likely have greater valuations than some of the most successful older entrepreneurs despite having zero net worth. This is because their valuations would take into account the overall growth in opportunities and leverage over the coming decades that would compound their potential in ways that older people wouldn't benefit from. This would strongly incentivize transfer of wealth from older generations to younger ones through investments.
